The Charge Against Me, As Best I Understand It
I.

On the Law, and the Door Meant Only for You

I do not believe the Law is hidden from us by accident, nor that it withholds itself out of cruelty exactly. I believe every person is given a door built to their own measure, and told, correctly, that it stands open — and that the terrible part is not the waiting, but discovering, too late, that the door was never for anyone else to walk through, and closes the moment you stop asking.

We assume the officials above us understand the system better than we do. I have never found evidence of this. I have only found more officials, each one certain that the answer sits with someone further up the stairs.

Before the Law stands a doorkeeper.
— "Before the Law"
II.

On Guilt, Held Without a Charge

A man can be arrested without having done anything wrong, and the worst of it is not the arrest — it is how quickly he begins searching his own memory for the crime, certain the court would not have bothered with him otherwise. I have watched this happen in myself more than once: guilt arriving first, and the reason for it arriving never.

We are trained from childhood to assume the accusation is more reliable than our own account of ourselves. I no longer think this a mark of good character. I think it is simply what a lifetime of small tribunals — a father, a school, an office — teaches a person to expect.

it is often safer to be found guilty of something named than innocent of something never specified — at least a named charge can, in principle, be answered.
III.

On the Father, the Family, and Waking Changed

I have written to my father a letter longer than most of my books, and I do not believe it was ever truly read, in the sense of being received rather than merely returned to sender with a verdict already decided. A child does not need to be struck to learn that his existence is a kind of trespass; he only needs to be measured, once, against a standard he was never told he was competing for.

I once wrote of a man who wakes to find he has become something his family can no longer recognize as belonging to them. I did not think this required much invention. I have felt, at my own table, exactly that unrecognized.

A cage went in search of a bird.
— from the notebooks
IV.

On Writing, and What a Book Is For

I do not write to be understood, and I am suspicious of books that leave a reader entirely comfortable at the end of them. A book that only confirms what a person already believed has not done its work. I want the kind of book that arrives, uninvited, like a summons — one you cannot simply set down and return to your life unchanged, because it has already gotten in.

A book must be the axe for the frozen sea within us.
— letter to Oskar Pollak
V.

On Freedom, and Why We Rarely Take It

I am not certain freedom is the relief people imagine it to be. A cell has walls, but it also has a shape you come to know by heart, and a great many people, offered the door, will ask first whether someone can confirm it is really permitted. I have built entire lives, my own included, around waiting for a permission that was never going to arrive from outside.

It is often safer to be in chains than to be free.
— The Trial
